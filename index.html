Concept 01 / 05
What is a Block?
Each "block" is a digital container storing a batch of verified transactions, a timestamp, and a cryptographic hash linking it to the previous block — forming a tamper-proof chain. Bitcoin blocks are created every ~10 minutes and hold up to ~2,700 transactions.
Data Structure
Concept 02 / 05
Public & Private Keys
Your public key is like a bank account number — share it freely to receive funds. Your private key is the password — lose it and you permanently lose access. A signature proves you own funds without revealing the private key, using elliptic curve cryptography (secp256k1).
Cryptography
Concept 03 / 05
Consensus Mechanisms
Proof of Work (PoW) — miners compete solving math puzzles to add blocks. Extremely secure but energy-intensive (Bitcoin). Proof of Stake (PoS) — validators are chosen based on staked coins. 99.95% more energy-efficient (Ethereum post-Merge). Both prevent double-spending without a central authority.
Network Agreement
Concept 04 / 05
Smart Contracts
Self-executing code stored on the blockchain that runs automatically when pre-set conditions are met. No lawyers, no middlemen — just code. Built in Solidity (Ethereum) or Rust (Solana), they power DeFi, NFTs, DAOs, and thousands of dApps. Once deployed, they cannot be altered — ever.
Self-Executing Code
Concept 05 / 05
The 51% Attack
To alter Bitcoin's history, you'd need to control 51% of all mining power worldwide — more computing than the top 500 supercomputers combined. The cost: $20B+ and rising every day. This is why Bitcoin's immutability is considered mathematically guaranteed. No hack in 15+ years of continuous operation.
Security Model

Frequently Asked Questions
What is blockchain technology in simple terms?

Imagine a Google Doc that thousands of people share — except nobody can edit or delete old entries, only add new ones. That's blockchain. It's a shared, digital ledger that records transactions across a global network of computers. No bank, government, or company controls it — the network itself enforces the rules through mathematics and code.

Every entry is called a block, and each block is locked to the previous one with a unique cryptographic code called a hash. This creates a chain of blocks — a blockchain. Change anything in one block and every block after it breaks. That's what makes it tamper-proof.

How does blockchain work step by step?

Step 1 — Initiation: A user creates a transaction (e.g. sending Bitcoin) and digitally signs it with their private key using cryptographic mathematics.

Step 2 — Broadcast: The signed transaction is immediately broadcast to thousands of nodes across the global peer-to-peer network.

Step 3 — Validation: Nodes verify the digital signature, confirm the sender has sufficient funds, and check for double-spending attempts.

Step 4 — Block Formation: Valid transactions are bundled into a new block containing a Merkle root, the previous block's hash, a timestamp, and a nonce.

Step 5 — Consensus: Miners (PoW) or validators (PoS) compete to finalize the block according to the network's consensus rules.

Step 6 — Finality: The confirmed block is added to the chain permanently. After 6 confirmations on Bitcoin (~60 minutes), the transaction is considered irreversible forever.

What is the difference between Bitcoin and blockchain?

Blockchain is the technology. Bitcoin is one application of that technology.

Think of blockchain like the internet — a foundational technology. Bitcoin is like email — one specific application built on it. Ethereum, Solana, Cardano, and thousands of other cryptocurrencies are all separate blockchains. They share the underlying principles (decentralization, cryptography, consensus) but differ in their design, speed, functionality, and purpose.

Bitcoin was specifically designed as a peer-to-peer electronic cash system. Ethereum was designed as a programmable blockchain for smart contracts. You can have blockchain without Bitcoin, but you cannot have Bitcoin without blockchain.

What is a smart contract and how does it work?

A smart contract is a self-executing program stored permanently on the blockchain. Think of it as a vending machine — you put in money, the machine checks the conditions (correct amount, item in stock), and automatically delivers what you paid for. No staff, no manager, no possibility of human error or corruption.

Smart contracts are written in programming languages like Solidity (Ethereum) or Rust (Solana). Once deployed, they cannot be altered. They run automatically when their conditions are triggered — powering DeFi loans, NFT minting, token swaps, insurance payouts, and more. Ethereum alone hosts over 50 million smart contracts.

Can blockchain be hacked?

The Bitcoin blockchain has never been successfully attacked in over 15 years. To alter the Bitcoin blockchain, an attacker would need to control more than 51% of the global hash rate — requiring more computational power than the world's 500 fastest supercomputers combined. The cost would exceed billions of dollars, and the attacker would destroy the very value they're trying to steal.

However, applications built on top of blockchains can be hacked — including smart contract bugs ($5B+ lost in DeFi), bridge vulnerabilities ($625M Ronin hack), exchange hacks (FTX collapse), and phishing attacks on individual wallets. The underlying blockchain protocol is extremely secure; it's the software built on it that carries risk.

What is Proof of Work vs Proof of Stake?

Proof of Work (PoW): Miners use powerful computers to solve complex mathematical puzzles. The winner earns the right to add the next block and receives a Bitcoin reward. This is extremely energy-intensive (~150 TWh/year for Bitcoin) but provides the strongest security through massive real-world resource expenditure. Used by Bitcoin and Litecoin.

Proof of Stake (PoS): Validators lock up (stake) their own cryptocurrency as collateral. They are randomly selected to validate blocks proportional to their stake. Malicious behavior results in their stake being "slashed." Uses 99.9% less energy than PoW. Used by Ethereum (since The Merge in 2022), Cardano, Solana, and Polkadot.

What is DeFi (Decentralized Finance)?

DeFi (Decentralized Finance) is an ecosystem of financial services — lending, borrowing, trading, earning interest — that operate entirely on blockchain through smart contracts, with no banks, brokers, or financial institutions required.

With DeFi you can: borrow $1M without a credit check (using crypto collateral), earn 5-20% interest on stablecoins, trade any token 24/7 on decentralized exchanges (DEXs), and receive a loan that auto-executes and auto-repays in a single transaction (flash loans). Over $80 billion in assets are locked in DeFi protocols as of 2025.

What are the real-world uses of blockchain technology?

Blockchain has proven real-world applications across many industries:

  • Finance: Cross-border payments in seconds for cents. Bitcoin ETFs with $50B+ AUM. JPMorgan's Onyx processing $1B/day in repo transactions.
  • Supply Chain: Walmart traces food contamination in 2.2 seconds using IBM Food Trust. Maersk saved $38M/year digitizing shipping documents.
  • Healthcare: MIT MedRec gives patients ownership of their medical records. Blockchain prevents $200B annual counterfeit medicine problem.
  • Real Estate: Georgia (country) stores all land titles on blockchain. Tokenization lets investors own fractional property for as little as $10.
  • Voting: Sierra Leone conducted the first blockchain election audit in 2018. DAOs govern $25B+ in assets through on-chain voting.
  • Digital Ownership: NFTs prove ownership of art, music, gaming items, domain names, and concert tickets on-chain permanently.

More Common Questions
What is tokenization of real-world assets (RWA)?

Tokenization converts real-world assets — property, bonds, commodities, equities, art — into blockchain tokens that represent fractional ownership. A $10M commercial property can be tokenized into 10M tokens worth $1 each. Investors worldwide can then buy $100 worth of that property without lawyers, paperwork, or geographic barriers.

RWA tokenization reached $10B+ in 2025 and is projected to reach $16 trillion by 2030 (Boston Consulting Group estimate). Key players include BlackRock (BUIDL fund on Ethereum), Franklin Templeton (tokenized money market fund), and Ondo Finance.

What is the difference between Layer 1 and Layer 2 blockchains?

Layer 1 (L1) is the base blockchain itself — Bitcoin, Ethereum, Solana, Avalanche. L1 handles consensus, security, and finality but is limited in throughput (Ethereum: ~15 TPS, Bitcoin: ~7 TPS).

Layer 2 (L2) networks sit on top of L1 and process transactions off-chain before batching them to L1 for finality. Optimistic Rollups (Optimism, Arbitrum) assume transactions are valid and only verify if challenged. ZK-Rollups (zkSync, StarkNet, Polygon zkEVM) use zero-knowledge proofs for instant mathematical verification. L2s achieve 1,000–100,000+ TPS while inheriting L1 security.

How is blockchain different from Bitcoin?

Blockchain is the underlying technology. Bitcoin is one application built using blockchain. The relationship is similar to the internet (technology) vs email (one application of the internet).

Bitcoin uses blockchain specifically as a ledger to record BTC transactions. But blockchain can record anything — smart contracts (Ethereum), supply chain data (IBM Food Trust), medical records (MedRec), land titles (Georgia), and more. Not all blockchains use Bitcoin, and not all crypto uses the same blockchain.

What is a crypto wallet and how does it work?

A crypto wallet doesn't actually store your crypto — your coins live on the blockchain. The wallet stores your private key, which proves ownership and allows you to sign transactions. Lose your private key = lose access forever. There's no customer support, no password reset, no bank to call.

Types: Hot wallets (MetaMask, Phantom) are connected to the internet — convenient but less secure. Cold wallets (Ledger, Trezor) are hardware devices that store private keys offline — the gold standard for security. The seed phrase (12-24 words) is the master backup that can regenerate any wallet. Guard it like your life savings depend on it — because they do.

What is DeFi and how is it different from traditional finance?

DeFi (Decentralized Finance) is a collection of financial services — lending, borrowing, trading, earning interest — built on smart contracts with no banks or brokers. Anyone with a wallet can access DeFi, 24/7, from anywhere in the world, with no KYC, no credit checks, and no account minimum.

Key DeFi protocols: Uniswap (DEX, $1T+ traded), Aave (lending/borrowing), MakerDAO (stablecoin DAI), Compound (yield), and Curve (stable swaps). The total value locked (TVL) in DeFi peaked at $180B in November 2021. The risk: smart contract bugs can lead to total fund loss — always verify audits before depositing.

What is an NFT and does it actually prove ownership?

An NFT (Non-Fungible Token) is a unique blockchain token that represents ownership of a specific digital or physical item. Unlike BTC (fungible — every coin is identical), NFTs are one-of-a-kind. Beeple's "Everydays" sold for $69M. Yuga Labs' Bored Ape Yacht Club built a $4B brand.

Important nuance: An NFT records ownership on-chain, but the actual image/file is typically stored on IPFS or a server. If that server goes down, the NFT points to nothing. True NFT ownership means the token ID is yours forever — but the associated media's permanence depends on the storage solution used.

Can blockchain be hacked or shut down?

The Bitcoin blockchain itself has never been successfully hacked in 15+ years. Each block uses SHA-256 encryption and is linked cryptographically to all previous blocks. Shutting it down would require simultaneously taking offline thousands of independent nodes across 100+ countries — practically impossible.

What can be hacked: smart contracts (Euler Finance: $197M, 2023), bridges (Ronin Bridge: $625M, 2022), centralized exchanges (FTX: $32B collapse, 2022), and phishing attacks stealing private keys. The blockchain layer is secure — the applications and human behavior on top of it are where the risk lies.

What is a stablecoin and why does it matter?

A stablecoin is a cryptocurrency pegged to a stable asset — usually the US dollar. They solve the volatility problem: you get blockchain's speed and borderlessness with a $1 = $1 guarantee. Types: Fiat-backed (USDT, USDC — backed by real dollars in a bank), Crypto-backed (DAI — overcollateralized with ETH), Algorithmic (failed spectacularly with TerraUSD/UST — $40B wiped in 72 hours, May 2022).

Total stablecoin market cap: $160B+. They're the backbone of DeFi, the primary settlement layer for crypto trades, and increasingly used for cross-border payments in high-inflation economies like Argentina, Turkey, and Nigeria.

What is a DAO and how does blockchain governance work?

A DAO (Decentralized Autonomous Organization) is an internet-native organization governed entirely by smart contracts and token-holder votes — no CEO, no board of directors. Members propose and vote on decisions using governance tokens. Code executes the results automatically.

Notable DAOs: MakerDAO (manages $5B+ DAI stablecoin), Uniswap DAO (governs $6B+ DEX), and Nouns DAO (auctions one NFT/day, uses treasury for community projects). DAOs collectively manage $25B+ in on-chain treasuries. Legal recognition is still evolving — Wyoming was first US state to legally recognize DAOs in 2021.
